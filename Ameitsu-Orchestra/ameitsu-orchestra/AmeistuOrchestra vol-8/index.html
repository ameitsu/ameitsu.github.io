# Scott of Antarctica: The ill-fated warrior against nature

A year-round Antarctic station became fully operational in November 1956, and occupying the southernmost point of the place where we stand since the furthest history: the south pole. Though Constructed by US National Science Foundation, the station wasn’t named using any great American figure’s name, instead they used two literally immortal names of humanity: Amundsen and Scott.

# The two warriors

The two men were both explorers, whose expertise were searching the frontier of the Earth. Amundsen was the first human to reach the south pole, and certainly he looked like the perfect person ever to accomplish this epic mission, since he had just navigated the icy waters of Canadian Northwest and Yukon. He seemed so well-prepared and fortunate, therefore people often focus on the deceased loser of the race to the south pole —— Captain Scott.

# Scott's voyage

Subsequent to Scott’s death, Amundsen mourned him and commented that Scott was the embodiment of all great characters of humans. Scott is famed for his unsuccessful expedition to the pole, and there is certainly a reason for it. Scott’s team encountered mechanical failures of their motorized sledges right after their departure, and facing challenges when you have just started is never better than failing it completely. After marching in the icy Antarctic plateaus in low mood for two months, they witnessed the scenario that none of the teammates would imagine: Amundsen arrived with a Norwegian flag already. This accounted for the second punch on their ambitions. The true despair occurred on their way back. With days passing by, the men soon realized the frightening fact that all supplies were in shortage. The explorers gradually slowed down the progress and eventually watched how fellow teammates die before them. And finally, the finale: Scott spent the final hours of himself to write the one last letter to his loved wife and son.

# What can we say

Since his death, opinions claiming Scott’s crude decision-making ability accounted for the tragedy were widely spread. Undoubtedly, it is a truth: Scott decided to use Yakut ponies and powered sledges rather than the safest option of skis and dogs, despite being warned by Amundsen prior to his departure. Nevertheless, he still deserved the title as the hero of Antarctica. The thing that powered Scott within was the spirit of endeavor and self-sacrificing. Regardless of the miserable beginning, the team managed to reach the destination. This is a character shared by all explorers from Christopher Columbus in the Atlantic to Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin up on the moon. Nothing burdensome could be accomplished without endeavor, not to mention traversing Antarctica. Soon after the men became aware about their destiny, they kept on and collected a total of 20 kilograms of precious stones that could possibly prove Antarctica’s geological history as a vibrant world rather than a snowy wasteland, saving considerable time and efforts for succeeding researchers. Resigning to their fate, the last two men, including Scott, breathed their final breath in a blizzard of 1912, yet they have already achieved more than attempting to reach the south pole: Not just researched Antarctica, but also explored the limit of human beings’ might. Failing never meant an unfortunate conclusion, those failed on their pathway of pursuing a greater good are the ones that ignited the flame of hope to guide the rest. And that is exactly the story of captain Robert Falcon Scott, the martyr on human’s way of conquering unknown.
